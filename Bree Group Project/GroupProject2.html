Whether it is magnificant temples, alternative fashion, or weird and wonderful food, Japan seems to be the place to go for those who want to experience a completely different culture. However, travel to this extraordinary country can get quite pricy. Here are some tips to help you save you hard-earned cash while still being able to experience Japan's wonders.

Accommodation

1. Capsule Hotels

Capsule hotels may look like they are straight out of a Sci-Fi, but these nifty pods not only are clean, but great for those who are looking for a place to spend for a night or two. A capsule hotel is a type of hotel developed in Japan that features many small bed-sized rooms known as capsules. Capsule hotels provide cheap, basic overnight accommodation for guests who do not require or who cannot afford larger, more expensive rooms offered by more conventional hotels. The advantages of these hotels is that you can meet other travellers, enjoy a good location at a small price, and experience a space-ship like night's sleep next to the neon lights of Tokyo. The only downside is that, being so close to your neighbours, it can get quite noisy, so make sure you bring earbuds! Sometimes, they are provided. 9 hours is a popular chain around Tokyo.

1. Hostels

Hostels are the go-to for students and young travellers all around the world, yet they come with costs. The closeness to others without the protective cocoon you get in the capsule hotel may be intrusive to some, and pleasing to others, yet it comes at an added risk. Japanese hostels, on the other hand, do not hold as much a negative stigma. They are clean and not as scruffy as others around the world, and have a warm an exciting atmosphere, with some having their own unique themes (and sometimes a bar!) yet I recommend this accommodation type for travellers in groups. Safety in numbers! Book and Bed Asakusa is a very cute choice. Be sure to check it out here!

Food

1. Ramen

Ramen is perhaps the most popular Japanese food in the West (aside from sushi!), but what exactly is it? Simply put, ramen is a Japanese noodle soup, with a combination of a rich flavoured broth, one of a variety of types of noodle and a selection of meats or vegetables, often topped with a boiled egg. In Japan ramen is considered a fast food, with many small restaurants and street vendors offering a warming bowl of this delicious soup.The dish of ramen is heavily regionalised, with different variations served in different areas of Japan, although all variations are based on the same broth and noodle concept. 3. Oden

Oden is a type of nabemono, consisting of several ingredients such as boiled eggs, daikon, konjac, and processed fishcakes stewed in a light, soy-flavored dashi broth. Oden was originally what is now commonly called misodengaku or simply dengaku; konjac or tofu was boiled and eaten with miso. Landmarks

1. Meiji Shrine, Harajuku

Meiji Shrine is a shrine dedicated to the deified spirits of Emperor Meiji and his consort, Empress Shoken. Located just beside the JR Yamanote Line's busy Harajuku Station, Meiji Shrine and the adjacent Yoyogi Park make up a large forested area within the densely built-up city. The spacious shrine grounds offer walking paths that are great for a relaxing stroll. The shrine was completed and dedicated to the Emperor Meiji and the Empress Shoken in 1920, eight years after the passing of the emperor and six years after the passing of the empress. The shrine was destroyed during the Second World War but was rebuilt shortly thereafter. This is not one of the most popular tourist attrations, but it is a hidden gem worth seeing.

2. Tokyo Metropolitan Building

The Tokyo Metropolitan Government Building in Shinjuku is often visited by tourists for its free observation decks which provide good panoramic views of Tokyo and beyond. The 243 meter tall building has two towers, and each houses an observatory at a height of 202 meters. It had been the tallest building in Tokyo until it was overtaken by the Midtown Tower in 2007. With favorable weather conditions, famous landmarks such as Mount Fuji, the Tokyo Skytree, Tokyo Tower, Meiji Shrine and the Tokyo Dome can be seen from the observatories. Each observatory has a cafe and a souvenir shop.

3. Ginza

The Ginza is Tokyo's most famous upmarket shopping, dining and entertainment district, featuring numerous department stores, boutiques, art galleries, restaurants, night clubs and cafes. One square meter of land in the district's center is worth over ten million yen, making it one of the most expensive real estate in Japan. It is where you can find the infamous $10 cups of coffee and where virtually every leading brand name in fashion and cosmetics has a presence. From 1612 to 1800, today's Ginza district was the site of a silver coin mint (Ginza means "silver mint" in Japanese), after which the district was eventually named. The Ginza evolved as an upmarket shopping district following the 1923 Great Kanto Earthquake.
